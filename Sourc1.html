Think back to the first time you had to consider how your career would change based on your visual problem. Or you might have been ready to enter college and needed to prepare yourself to achieve academic excellence in an environment designed for a visual world. Let's first lay some ground work that will later be important. Visual impairment affects each person differently. On the one end of the spectrum, some people are completely blind from birth. On the other end of the spectrum, some people may be in there 50's living with Low-Vision, 20/200 which is the largest E on the standardized eye chart. For me, I have Retinitis Pigmentosa. So the visual acuity in my "good" eye is about 20/30 and 20/80 in my "bad" eye. However, in the periphery of my vision I'm totally blind in both eyes. When I think about my career, often my inner narrative goes something like this

"I want to continue in my current career. My customer(s) are satisfied with my work, and I'm satisfied with the work I have delivered. I've worked very hard for many years to master the techniques used to deliver high quality tools and products. But alas, I've excelled at my career goals because (up until recently) I could see fairly well. All of my work is based on using a computer as I'm a software developer. In this sense my career world is entirely visual. I use specialized software that makes it possible to build software, so the typical use case of screen reading software geared toward reading emails and writing word documents probably will not cut it for me. How can I possibly make a transition from a visual computer experience of 20 plus years, to a non-visual auditory computer experience and still maintain the level of mastery and expertise I expect from myself?"

I've tested several screen readers. Every time I try using them I get so frustrated after about 5 minutes of use. Frustrated NOT because I think it's bad or poorly designed software, but because transitioning to a non-visual way of working is a task that currently seems to difficult to overcome.
